Surface Roughness, Log Wind Profile, and Geometric Mean Heights

1. Neutral Log Profile

Under neutral stratification (ignore stability corrections):
U(z)=u∗κ ln⁡ ⁣(zz0),U(z) = \frac{u_*}{\kappa}\,\ln\!\left(\frac{z}{z_0}\right),U(z)=κu∗​​ln(z0​z​),
where:

  • u∗u_*u∗​: friction velocity
  • κ≈0.40\kappa \approx 0.40κ≈0.40: von Kármán constant
  • z0z_0z0​: aerodynamic roughness length

2. Averaging Velocities Across Multiple Heights

Let heights z1,…,zNz_1,\dots,z_Nz1​,…,zN​ and corresponding mean horizontal speeds Ui=U(zi)U_i = U(z_i)Ui​=U(zi​). Then:
Uˉ=1N∑i=1NU(zi)=u∗κ[1N∑i=1Nln⁡ ⁣(ziz0)]=u∗κln⁡ ⁣((∏i=1Nzi)1/Nz0).\bar{U} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N U(z_i) = \frac{u_*}{\kappa}\left[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ln\!\left(\frac{z_i}{z_0}\right)\right] = \frac{u_*}{\kappa}\ln\!\left(\frac{( \prod_{i=1}^N z_i )^{1/N}}{z_0}\right).Uˉ=N1​i=1∑N​U(zi​)=κu∗​​[N1​i=1∑N​ln(z0​zi​​)]=κu∗​​ln(z0​(∏i=1N​zi​)1/N​).

Uˉ=U(zg).\bar{U} = U(z_g).Uˉ=U(zg​).
Conclusion: The arithmetic mean of the velocities equals the log-profile velocity evaluated at the geometric mean of heights, not at the arithmetic mean height. Any interpretation of “representative” height for the averaged velocity should use zgz_gzg​.

3. Implication for Roughness and Friction Velocity Estimation

If one regress UiU_iUi​ against ln⁡zi\ln z_ilnzi​:
Ui=Aln⁡zi+B,A=u∗κ,B=−u∗κln⁡z0.U_i = A \ln z_i + B,\quad A = \frac{u_*}{\kappa},\quad B = -\frac{u_*}{\kappa}\ln z_0.Ui​=Alnzi​+B,A=κu∗​​,B=−κu∗​​lnz0​.
Thus:

  • u∗=κAu_* = \kappa Au∗​=κA.
  • z0=exp⁡ ⁣(−B/A)z_0 = \exp\!\left(-B/A\right)z0​=exp(−B/A).

Averaging velocities first and pairing with an arithmetic mean height biases the inferred z0z_0z0​ (systematically misrepresents the intercept), especially when heights span orders of magnitude.

4. Recommended Procedure

  1. Use individual height measurements (do not pre-average across heights).
  2. Restrict to neutral periods (e.g., small |Obukhov length|; apply stability corrections otherwise: add ψm(z/L)\psi_m(z/L)ψm​(z/L) term).
  3. Perform linear regression of UUU vs ln⁡z\ln zlnz.
  4. Extract u∗u_*u∗​ and z0z_0z0​ from slope and intercept.
  5. If reporting a single “representative height” for an averaged velocity, specify zgz_gzg​, not zˉ\bar{z}zˉ.

5. Practical Notes

  • Displacement height ddd: For tall vegetation, replace zzz with z−dz-dz−d everywhere; geometric mean uses zi−dz_i-dzi​−d.
  • Quality control: Exclude low-level sensor data if within roughness sublayer (~first 2–3 z0z_0z0​).
  • Uncertainty: Propagate regression standard errors to u∗u_*u∗​ and z0z_0z0​; log-space linearity simplifies error estimates.

6. Summary

Neutral-layer logarithmic dependence means velocity averaging across levels maps to geometric mean height; roughness and friction velocity estimates should honor the log-linear structure, avoiding arithmetic-height substitutions.

12. Appendix: Reflections on κ\kappaκ, γ\gammaγ, and Notation

Historical values:

  • Classical “consensus” κ≈0.40±0.01\kappa \approx 0.40\pm 0.01κ≈0.40±0.01.
  • Businger–Dyer formulations often used κ≈0.38\kappa \approx 0.38κ≈0.38, emerging from early micrometeorological datasets (instrument tilt, flow distortion, limited Reynolds number).
  • Modern high-Re boundary layer and channel/pipe experiments cluster near 0.39–0.410.39\text{–}0.410.39–0.41.

Relation to Euler–Mascheroni constant:

  • Euler’s constant γ≈0.57721\gamma \approx 0.57721γ≈0.57721; status: irrationality and transcendence remain unproven.
  • Numerical coincidence: 1−γ≈0.422781-\gamma \approx 0.422781−γ≈0.42278 sits near the empirical range of κ\kappaκ; 1/γ≈1.7321/\gamma \approx 1.7321/γ≈1.732 is not relevant.
  • No accepted derivation linking κ\kappaκ to a simple function of γ\gammaγ; proximity of 1−γ1-\gamma1−γ is viewed as incidental.

Digamma function vs. ABL stability function:

  • Mathematical digamma: ψ(x)=ddxln⁡Γ(x)\psi(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\ln\Gamma(x)ψ(x)=dxd​lnΓ(x).
  • Boundary-layer stability corrections also traditionally denoted ψm(z/L),ψh(z/L)\psi_m(z/L), \psi_h(z/L)ψm​(z/L),ψh​(z/L), causing confusion with ψ(x)\psi(x)ψ(x) in special-function contexts.
  • In this document ϕ(z/L)\phi(z/L)ϕ(z/L) / ψm\psi_mψm​ are atmospheric; digamma is explicitly “digamma” or ψ(⋅)\psi(\cdot)ψ(⋅) when needed in gamma-function derivations.

Estimation reflections:

  • Library work on digamma/gamma identities (e.g., channel-flow integrals yielding logarithmic velocity profiles) can motivate indirect κ\kappaκ estimation by fitting the mean shear profile to forms involving ψ\psiψ differences, but atmospheric practice favors direct regression UUU vs. ln⁡z\ln zlnz.
  • Neutral, high-Re surface-layer data with careful tilt correction and displacement height treatment reduce spread in κ\kappaκ.

Reasons for spread (0.38 vs 0.40):

  • Sensor alignment and flow distortion.
  • Incomplete exclusion of non-neutral stratification.
  • Roughness sublayer contamination.
  • Limited averaging times (non-stationarity).
  • Displacement height misestimation shifting slope.

Notation discipline:

  • Reserve γ\gammaγ for Euler–Mascheroni constant, avoid reusing for turbulence parameters.
  • Use κ\kappaκ solely for von Kármán; atmospheric ψm\psi_mψm​ kept distinct from digamma ψ(x)\psi(x)ψ(x).
